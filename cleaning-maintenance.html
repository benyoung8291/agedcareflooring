The Problem We Solve

Most aged care floors aren't worn out. They're just badly cleaned.

Every year we get called into facilities that are quoting replacement budgets for floors that don't actually need replacing. They look terrible, sure. The corridors are matted. The dining room vinyl looks dull and patchy. The carpet in the lounge has a permanent traffic shadow. But pull up a tile and the backing is fine. The vinyl seam welds are still intact. The carpet pile is still there underneath, just buried under months of soil that the daily clean has been pushing around rather than lifting.

A floor that's been properly maintained from day one will get fifteen to twenty years out of its design life. A floor that's been mopped with the wrong chemistry, vacuumed with the wrong pattern, and never had a proper restoration clean will look exhausted at five years and need replacing at eight. The replacement cost is the visible number, but the real cost is everything in between — the slip-rating drift, the resident complaints, the audit findings, the staff embarrassment when a family member walks into a room.

Cleaning and maintenance is where the durability of a flooring spec actually lives. We do both sides — replacement when it's genuinely needed, and the cleaning programme that means it doesn't need to happen as often.

How We Run An Aged Care Site

Two service models. Pick the one that fits your facility.

When we pick up a new aged care customer, we tend to fall into one of two models. Either is fine. The first visit is on us, and after walking the floors we'll tell you which one suits your setup — and we'll be honest if it's the model that earns us less revenue per facility, because in a lot of aged care it is.

Model 1: Train Your Existing Cleaner

Aged care facilities almost always have a general cleaning contract already in place — or an in-house housekeeping team — running the daily clean across bathrooms, common areas, corridors and kitchens. In most cases that team isn't set up for specialist carpet care, doesn't have the right chemistry, and doesn't know the nuances of an aged care carpet. We come in, train their team on the right method for the carpets that are actually on the floor, supply the correct chemistry and systems, and stay on as ongoing support. Your team self-delivers the maintenance work. We get called in for the periodic professional clean, restoration work, and anything beyond the routine.

The Honest Bit

We earn less per facility on the training model. We do it anyway.

Doing the cleaning ourselves at full scope is the more profitable model for us. There's no getting around that. If we turn up weekly or fortnightly and run the full specialist regime across every site, the revenue per facility is higher and delivery is simpler from our side.

But that model doesn't work for every facility, and aged care is the sector where that reality bites hardest. Many facilities operate on tight margins, particularly in the not-for-profit and mid-tier provider space. A specialist cleaning contract at full scope isn't something every budget can absorb. And the residents living in those facilities don't get to choose their floor covering or their cleaning regime — they live with whatever the facility can afford to deliver.

So we offer the partnership model. We train the people already on site, give them the systems, supply the chemistry, come back for the periodic professional work, and accept that we earn less per facility in exchange for more facilities having properly maintained floors and more residents living in spaces that look the way they should. We still charge for the training, the systems, the chemistry, and the periodic visits. We just don't gatekeep the knowledge or pretend carpet maintenance is more mysterious than it is so we can charge a premium for the mystery.

Slip-Rating Preservation

The cleaning programme is half of your slip compliance

Most aged care floors that fail an AS 4663 in-situ slip test didn't lose their slip resistance because the product was wrong. They lost it because of how the floor was cleaned.

Wrong Chemistry

Acrylic floor polishes, oil-based dressings, and silicone-bearing cleaners leave a film that progressively reduces friction. A floor passes its installation test, then drops a P-rating each year.

Wax And Polish Build-Up

Repeated layering of polish without proper stripping creates a glossy, slippery surface that's nothing like the floor as installed. Common on dining-room and corridor vinyl.

Residue From Detergents

Cleaners that aren't fully rinsed leave a sticky residue that traps soil and reduces friction. The floor looks clean but tests poorly.

Annual In-Situ Testing

We arrange AS 4663 wet-pendulum testing through NATA-accredited labs, document it for your compliance file, and rectify with the right cleaning programme rather than premature replacement.

Approved Chemistry Lists

We supply a written list of cleaning products approved for the specific floors in your facility, with SDS sheets ready for staff training and audit submission.
